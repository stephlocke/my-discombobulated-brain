Anxiety is one of the most common mental health experiences, affecting people in many different ways. For some it appears as a persistent hum of worry in the background of daily life. For others it can feel sudden and overwhelming. Neither experience is unusual, and neither means something is fundamentally wrong with you.

## What anxiety actually is

At its core, anxiety is your body's threat-response system doing what it was designed to do. When your brain perceives danger, it triggers a cascade of physical and mental changes, including a faster heartbeat, shallow breathing, and heightened alertness. In situations of genuine danger, this is genuinely useful.

The difficulty arises when that same system activates in response to situations that are not life-threatening, such as a difficult conversation, a looming deadline, or an unfamiliar social situation. Your body reacts as if the threat is real, because to your nervous system, it feels real.

## Common signs of anxiety

Anxiety can show up physically, emotionally, and behaviourally. You might notice:

- Tension in your chest, shoulders, or jaw
- Difficulty sleeping or waking up with a sense of dread
- Racing or looping thoughts
- Avoiding situations that feel overwhelming
- Irritability or difficulty concentrating

These experiences vary from person to person, and they can shift over time.

## Some approaches that can help

There is no single solution that works for everyone, but the following are worth exploring:

**Grounding techniques** can help interrupt the anxiety spiral by bringing your attention back to the present moment. The 5-4-3-2-1 method, noticing five things you can see, four you can touch, three you can hear, two you can smell, and one you can taste, is one example.

**Paced breathing** activates the parasympathetic nervous system and can reduce the intensity of a physical anxiety response. Breathing in for four counts, holding for four, and out for six is a commonly used pattern.

**Talking to someone** is not a sign of weakness. Whether that is a trusted friend, a peer support group, or a trained therapist, being heard makes a difference.
