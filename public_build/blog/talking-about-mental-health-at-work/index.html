Talking About Mental Health at Work

June 10, 2024
3 min read

Opening up about mental health in a workplace setting can feel daunting. This post explores why those conversations matter and how to approach them with confidence.

Talking about mental health at work remains difficult for many people. Fear of judgement, concerns about job security, and uncertainty about how a manager might respond all play a part. Yet the cost of staying silent, both to individuals and to organisations, is significant.

Why the silence persists

Many people have absorbed the message, often unspoken, that the workplace is not an appropriate place to discuss personal struggles. Professionalism, in many settings, has come to mean keeping difficult feelings out of sight. This is changing, but slowly.

The result is that people often manage significant mental health challenges while performing as though everything is fine. That takes a considerable amount of energy, and over time it tends to make things worse rather than better.

The case for opening up

When people feel safe enough to be honest about what they are experiencing, several things become possible. Reasonable adjustments can be made before a situation becomes a crisis. Workloads can be redistributed if needed. A person can get support earlier, when it is often more effective.

For organisations, a culture where mental health is talked about openly tends to see lower rates of long-term sickness absence and higher levels of trust and retention.

How to start the conversation

There is no single right way, but a few things tend to help:

Choose the right moment. A brief one-to-one, rather than a busy meeting room, is usually more appropriate. If you are speaking to a manager, asking for a private catch-up gives them time to be present.

You do not need to share everything. It is reasonable to say that you have been finding things difficult without giving a full account of what is happening. You might say something like: “I wanted to let you know I’ve been going through a difficult period and it has been affecting me. I wanted to flag it rather than let it build up.”

Know your rights. Under the Equality Act 2010, if a mental health condition has a substantial and long-term effect on your ability to carry out normal daily activities, it may be considered a disability, and your employer has a duty to make reasonable adjustments.

If you manage others

The most important thing a manager can do is create the conditions in which someone might feel comfortable having this conversation. That means responding without alarm, listening without immediately trying to fix, and following up.

If you are in crisis or need urgent support, please contact the Samaritans on 116 123 (available 24 hours a day, free to call) or text SHOUT to 85258.
